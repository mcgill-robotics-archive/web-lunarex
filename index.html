The NASA Lunabotics Mining Competition

The NASA Lunabotics Mining Competition is a showcase of student excellence in Science, Technology, Engineering and Mathematics, encouraging students around the world to develop solutions to tomorrow’s problems. Each team builds either a remote-controlled or autonomous robot whose goal is to collect and deposit as much simulated regolith (lunar soil) as possible in ten minutes. In 2013, we performed better than ever, collecting 10.3 kg and placing 12th overall. Design challenges include considerations of the physical properties of regolith, the vacuum environment, and penalties for weight and data transmission. The competition attracts high-performing students from around the world. In 2013, over 700 students from 50 universities across 5 continents came together to compete at the Kennedy Space Centre in Florida

Innovate first.

El Gordo

In 2013 we designed a robot unlike any that the competition had ever seen before. This first iteration embodied our theme of innovation before optimization. “El Gordo” may have been one of the heaviest robots, but it was constantly the focus of everybody’s attention as we demonstrated proof-of-concept technology such as 3D-printed deformable wheels, sophisticated steering, and a carbon-fiber auger.

We ambitiously attempted full autonomy, an accomplishment never achieved at the NASA Lunabotics Mining Competition. El Gordo set a McGill record, placing 12th overall.

MDA
Based out of Richmond, British Columbia, MDA is a major contributor towards Canada’s international impact on science and engineering. Some of their work includes satellite surveillance and communications (including RADARSAT) as well as advancement in the field of robotics. Notably, they have greatly assisted with the International Space Station and space shuttle programs. They are also responsible for the Canadarm, and the recently launched NASA Curiosity Rover features the MDA designed and constructed Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer. We greatly appreciate all the support that they have given our team!

Metso
Metso is a global supplier of technology and services to customers in the process industries, including mining, construction, pulp and paper, power, and oil and gas. Its 30,000 professionals based in over 50 countries deliver sustainability and profitability to customers worldwide.

They have three reporting segments: Mining and Construction, consisting of Minerals Processing Solutions, Crushing and Screening Equipment and Services business lines, Automation, consisting of Process Automation Systems, Flow Control and Services business lines and Pulp, Paper and Power, consisting of Paper, Fiber, Power and Services business lines.

Schaeffler
From vehicles to aviation and aerospace, Schaeffler Technologies is a leading manufacturer of precision bearings worldwide. Incorporating well-known brands such as INA, FAG and LuK, Schaeffler's machined needle and cylindrical roller bearings are essential components of virtually every automobile on the road today. In addition to manufacturing high-precision bearings for the aerospace industry, Schaeffler also specializes in large-scale applications, such as the large diameter bearings found on wind turbines.

Schaeffler Technologies has generously supplied McGill LunarEx with high-precision ball bearings that are crucial to the smooth operation of our active suspension and bucket dumping mechanisms. Thank you Schaeffler for all your support!

Stratasys
Stratasys is a worldwide 3D printing services company that offers cutting-edge resources in rapid-prototyping. The ability to quickly produce physical prototypes or manufactured goods with only a CAD file is making waves around the manufacturing industry. Stratasys stands apart from the competition with innovative new solutions such as the ability to print in multiple materials with a range of properties. We are delighted to have their support as we learn to use this novel technology to manufacture our wheels. These custom-designed "tires" will offer superior traction that will greatly improve our performance at the NASA Lunabotics Mining Competition.

Willow Garage
Willow Garage develops hardware and open source software for personal robotics applications. They believe that robotics is the future and they use their expertise to contribute to this vision by investing time and resources in ways that will promote the efficiency of personal robotics and the development community. The McGill LunarEx Team used their Robot Operating System which is an easy-to-use way of integrating all of the components of our robot in a way that is both reliable and customizable. Willow Garage is also responsible for the OpenCV computer vision library and Point Cloud Library, all of which use an open source software license. We thank Willow Garage for their contribution and encourage you to check out their projects!

LunarEx in the Past

McGill LunarEx Robotics has grown a lot since its founding in 2007. Find out more about how we got to where we are today.

2008
It all started in the Fall of 2007. NASA founded the Centennial Regolith Collection Challenge, a competition open to both universities as well as industry to spur the development new technology. McGill put a team together to compete. It was an exercise in perseverance. Being a new idea, there was a constant struggle associated with getting established, finding a place to work, recruiting members to join, convincing sponsors to finance a project with no history, designing a machine with nothing to build on, and most importantly, learning how to work in a team and produce something tangible. A screw-operated scoop system was built, with a single scooping arm that was actuated by a long lead screw. The team then made its way to California for the 2008 NASA Centennial Challenge. Shipping delays and customs issues between the Canada-US border caused the team to miss the chance of participating in the event at that year, however, this only made the team want to be a part of this experience even further and pushed for the continuation of this project in 2009.
2009
The second year for the project was simply about making it back to the 2009 NASA Centennial Challenge and finally getting a chance to see the excavator in action. With another year to prepare, the team could make use of all the experience it had accumulated, from technical design skills to more practical aspects like more effective collaboration between members. With a new and improved lunar excavator, the team made it to the 2009 NASA Centennial Challenge and got its chance to participate and display the final system in action. The two-robot system featured a wheeled mobile collection module that resembled a small digger and a separate lifting module to deposit the collected regolith into the bin. Software problems got in the way at the last minute, but the team demonstrated remote controlled regolith collection and deposition efficacy. The centennial challenge was won and declared solved that same year, by the industrial team "Paul's robotics".
2010
Inspired by the success and interest in the centennial challenge, NASA founded a new robotic mining competition called Lunabotics, but only opened it to up to teams from universities in the United States. McGill LunarEx Robotics, however, could not be stopped. Borrowing the idea from a snow blower, the team designed an auger mechanism for the collection of regolith. A year was spent running tests on a pulling rig to evaluate the proof of concept. This continuous excavation mechanism showed the potential to move a lot of regolith. A paper was published based on the findings, and the team was eager to demonstrate its capabilities on a full-scale robot.
2011
In 2011 the NASA Lunabotics Mining Competition opened its doors to international teams! This was the first year of implementing an auger collection mechanism on a robot. The system featured a one-stage, high RPM auger system with a lifting bucket. The robot passed technical inspection, but during a trial run before the competition, irreparable damage of linear actuators and of our tracked propulsion system forced a shutdown.
2012
In 2012, McGill LunarEx Robotics continued to innovate. A two-stage auger was developed that operates at low-RPM to minimize dust and safety hazards. A prototype was developed to test this excavation mechanism that nobody had ever tried before. Unfortunately, tests revealed that the two-stage auger was ineffective at collecting regolith. Insufficient time remained to redesign the system for the competition.
2013
As many of the team members graduated, McGill LunarEx Robotics took the opportunity to start over with a blank slate. 50 students were recruited and remained engaged throughout the year to build the most successful Lunabot ever. High scores in the static events such as the design presentation and systems engineering report earned us 12th place overall.
2014
After a strong history of international collaboration at the Lunabotics Mining Competition, NASA has decided to restrict the competition to only teams from the United States. There is hope for a Canadian competition, but a large amount of the team’s resources have been diverted into a new challenge. Find out more about McGill Robotics.
